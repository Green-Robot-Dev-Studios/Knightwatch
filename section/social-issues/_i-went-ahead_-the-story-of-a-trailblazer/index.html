Maya G. /  Fri, 24 Feb 2023

Since it's Black History Month, I thought it'd be a good time to discuss one of the most notable Black Canadian trailblazers in our recent history: Violet Pauline King Henry. She was born in 1929 and died rather young, in 1982, but had such a large and ever-lasting impact during her 53 years. She is most commonly known today as the first Black female lawyer in Canada, but achieved many other major milestones, such as being the first Black Canadian to obtain a law degree in Alberta, as well as the first Black person admitted to the Alberta Bar and the first woman appointed to an executive position with the YMCA (within the United States).


At a young age, King knew she wanted to pursue a legal career, to the point where her Grade 12 yearbook caption read: “Violet wants to be a criminal lawyer". She became president of the Girls’ Association the same year, when she attended Crescent Heights High School. She was said to be always studying during sleepovers, while everyone else went to sleep, according to one of her close friends.


She later attended the University of Alberta, and out of 142 students, King was one of only three women in the Faculty of Law, as well as one of four students to receive an Executive “A” gold ring during Colour Night (an annual event that celebrates students’ contributions to the University). She was also vice-president of the students’ union and representative to the National Federation of Canadian University Students, while still getting incredible grades. Finally, in 1998, King was inducted to the National YMCA Hall of Fame.


Not only did she prove her dedication to her studies and how hardworking she was again and again, but it's because of her that countless students around the world now have access to more opportunities (that they should have gotten in the first place) for education and prosperity in their chosen careers. It goes without saying that Violet King's name will forever be mentioned in the same sentence as the Canadian court of law—as it rightly should be—and that she's an inspiring trailblazer with a story that should be told to everyone who will listen, as well as everyone who refuses to.


"People told me it wasn't a good idea for a girl to be a lawyer, particularly a coloured girl - so I went ahead (and proved them wrong)."


  • Violet Pauline King Henry (1929 - 1982)
